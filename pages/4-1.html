Definition (MGF)

The moment generating function of a random variable X is

$$M_X(t)=\mathbb{E}\!\left[e^{tX}\right]= \begin{cases} \displaystyle \sum_{x} e^{t x}\, f_X(x) & \text{if } X \text{ has a pmf},\\[6pt] \displaystyle \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{t x}\, f_X(x)\, dx & \text{if } X \text{ has a pdf}. \end{cases}$$

1. Computing moments (mean, variance, etc.) is much easier with MGF

To compute a moment using a PDF/PMF, you often need an integral or an infinite sum. With the MGF you only need derivatives.

Example: If \(X\) is Poisson with mean \(\lambda\), then $$M_X(t)=\exp\!\big(\lambda\,(e^{t}-1)\big).$$ The mean is \(M_X'(0)\), the second moment is \(M_X''(0)\), and so on.

2. Finding the PDF of a sum of random variables can be easier

Finding the PDF of \(Z=X+Y\) is sometimes easier if you know the MGF (or characteristic function), since for independent variables you can multiply MGFs:

$$M_Z(t)=M_X(t)\,M_Y(t).$$
